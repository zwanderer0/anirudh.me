Seven(by)Five Tessellations

Kinematic expandable surfaces from computational geometry

Advisor
Prof. Erik Demaine, MIT CSAIL
Context
MIT CSAIL, computational geometry research

Problem

A flat sheet that expands into a curved surface without stretching, tearing, or adding material. That is the core constraint. The project started from Chuck Hoberman's expandable Cartesian grid linkage – a mechanism where rigid tiles connect at nodes that allow rotation but not translation. The question was whether the same principle could produce surfaces with controlled curvature: not just bigger and smaller, but shaped.

Fabrication

Each tile is laser-cut from stainless steel sheet with living hinges at the connection points. The joints use a ball-and-socket geometry that provides three rotational degrees of freedom per node while constraining linear motion to a single axis. This gives the assembled surface the ability to bend in one curvature direction while remaining rigid in the other – a property that turns out to be useful for deployable structures, architectural skins, and responsive building envelopes.

Prototype

The prototype consists of roughly 800 hand-cast components assembled over five days. At full expansion, the surface area increases by a factor determined by the joint geometry – in this case, approximately 1.4x. The design was computed using the folding algorithms developed in Prof. Erik Demaine's group at MIT CSAIL, where the mathematical constraints of rigid origami meet the practical demands of material fabrication.

Thesis

The research interest is in the gap between computational geometry and physical material. The math says a surface can fold a certain way; the steel and the joints say otherwise, until the tolerances are right. That negotiation – between the ideal fold and the real hinge – is where the interesting engineering lives.
